Every brief for a physical space eventually asks the same question: how many people walked past? It is the wrong question, and the difference between the right question and the wrong one determines what gets built.

## Traffic is not attention

A person walking past an installation has donated a glance. A person standing in front of it, moving their hand to see what happens, calling a friend over — that person has donated a memory. Spaces are judged, funded, and remembered on the second kind of donation, but they are usually measured on the first.

The measurement error produces a design error. If footfall is the metric, the rational move is a bigger, brighter, louder surface — a billboard with better resolution. If participation is the metric, the rational move is a system that gives one person a reason to act, and makes the action visibly matter.

## What participation requires from a space

People participate when three things are true:

1. **The invitation is legible.** They can tell, without instructions, that the space will respond to them. A floor that ripples underfoot teaches its own interaction in one step.
2. **The response is theirs.** A reaction that would have happened anyway is scenery. A reaction that is unmistakably caused by *this person, right now* creates ownership — and ownership is what people photograph and retell.
3. **The stakes are gentle.** Nobody wants to fail publicly in a lobby. Interactions that cannot be done wrong invite the hesitant, not just the confident.

These are engineering requirements, not copywriting. Legibility is a sensing and latency problem. Ownership is an identity and state problem. Gentle stakes are an interaction design problem.

## An audience is a design failure

The XSMITHS philosophy states it as a belief: people should participate — not as an audience, but as the reason the space exists at all. Families like [INTERACT](/xsmiths/products/interact) and [PLAY](/xsmiths/products/play) exist precisely for this: to convert the person walking past into the person the room is about.

The honest measure of an installation is not how many people saw it. It is how many people it saw — and answered.
