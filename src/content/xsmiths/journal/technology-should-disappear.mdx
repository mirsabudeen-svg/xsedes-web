The most honest test of experience technology happens about four seconds after a visitor meets it. In those four seconds, one of two things occurs: the person starts thinking about what the room just did — or they start thinking about how the room did it.

The second outcome is a failure, even when the technology is impressive. Especially when the technology is impressive.

## The machinery problem

Interactive installations attract engineers, and engineers are proud of mechanisms. The temptation is to surface the mechanism: show the sensor, celebrate the AI, name the framework on a plaque. But a visitor who is decoding the machinery is no longer inside the experience. They have stepped outside it to inspect the plumbing.

Museums have understood this for a century. Nobody lights a gallery so that visitors admire the light fixtures. The light exists so the work can be seen. Experience technology deserves the same discipline: sensors, show control, and models are fixtures. The response is the work.

## What disappearing actually requires

Making technology disappear is not a styling decision. It is an engineering budget spent on things visitors will never consciously notice:

- **Latency.** A response that arrives half a second late reads as a malfunction, and a malfunction makes people look for the machine.
- **Placement.** A camera at eye level announces itself. Sensing that is engineered into the architecture does not.
- **Judgment.** A system that reacts to everything proves it is a system. A system that responds only when the response is useful feels like the room simply noticed.

None of this photographs well for a portfolio. All of it decides whether the person in the space feels wonder or feels surveilled.

## The residue

What should remain after the technology disappears? Emotion. A person who cannot explain what just happened, but wants to bring someone back to show them — that is the deliverable. The machinery's highest achievement is to be forgotten by everyone except the team maintaining it.
